# Text Scanning Patterns: Eyetracking Evidence

On the web, people don't read every word on a page — they scan. They naturally attempt to be efficient and put in the least possible work for achieving their goal. They have learned that scanning can deliver almost the same amount of information as reading, but with significantly less time and effort.

## Scanning Patterns

How people read on the web is highly contingent upon:

- Their task
- Their assumptions from previous experiences with the internet, site, or brand
- The page layout
- The type of page content (e.g., text versus images)

Eyetracking research has identified multiple scanning patterns for webpages. The **4 patterns people use to scan text** on the web, listed in increasing order of effectiveness (worst to best):

1. F-pattern
2. Spotted pattern
3. Layer-cake pattern
4. Commitment pattern

Note: other patterns may be used when page content involves many images (e.g., the zigzag pattern and exhaustive review).

## F-Pattern

In the absence of subheadings and bullets, users tend to fixate on words toward the beginning of lines and toward the top of the page. This scanning behavior results in an eyetracking pattern resembling the capital letter F.

In left-to-right languages, text on the left and towards the top of the page is read more than text on the right or towards the bottom. In right-to-left languages the pattern is mirrored vertically, with more attention spent on the right side of the page.

## Spotted Pattern

The spotted scanning pattern involves fixating on specific words or chunks of words spread throughout the page. Users choose words for one of two reasons:

1. **They visually stand out** because they are styled differently (e.g., links, differently colored words, bolded words, bulleted lists).
2. **They resemble a word the user is looking for** to accomplish the current task (for example, capital letters for an address, digits for numeric information).

The spotted pattern is slightly more effective than the F-pattern if designers do a good job naming links, making important words look different from body text, and creating bulleted lists.

## Layer-Cake Scanning Pattern

The layer-cake scanning pattern consists of fixations placed mostly on the page's headings and subheadings, with few fixations on the body text in between — until users locate the heading they are interested in, at which point they usually read the accompanying body text below.

In an eyetracking heatmap or gaze plot, this pattern looks like horizontal stripes with blank spaces between them, resembling a layer cake.

Aside from reading almost every word, the layer-cake pattern is by far the most effective way users can scan pages.

## Commitment Pattern

The commitment pattern demonstrates traditional reading, not scanning. Users fixate on all or most content words in a text passage. This pattern usually occurs when users are very interested or highly motivated to read the content (e.g., studying for a test, or reading return instructions on a specific site).

The commitment pattern usually leads to the best comprehension, even though it is the most time-consuming.

**Important caveat:** Even for the commitment pattern, text comprehension is improved when content is chunked and calls out its main points in subheadings. Knowing users may want to read certain content does not justify loading pages with walls of text.

People are motivated to commit when they:

- Know and trust the source
- Are loyal to the brand
- Believe they are in the best place to find the information (e.g., because they received a referral, the page title matches their exact need, or the SERP description matches their thinking)

## Summary

When writing, editing, or organizing text on a webpage or in an app, how content is presented will favor one of the four text-scanning patterns. Most users will read very little from a wall of text. Support them by:

- Chunking content into sections and bulleted lists
- Using meaningful subheadings
- Applying special visual styling for keywords
