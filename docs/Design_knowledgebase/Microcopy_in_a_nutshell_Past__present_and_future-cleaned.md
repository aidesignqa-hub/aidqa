# Microcopy in a Nutshell: Past, Present and Future

Microcopy is the small text snippets that guide users through websites, apps, and other digital experiences. Its common goal: to help people navigate through a digital product or service as smoothly as possible. Microcopy uses words to solve problems.

## What is Microcopy?

### Types of Microcopy in Digital Interfaces

- **Onboarding copy** — guides users when they sign up for a new product or service
- **Error messages** (including 404 pages) — informs users of a problem (e.g., incorrect password entered)
- **Success messages** — confirms a user has completed a task
- **Tooltips** — appear on hover or touch to explain functionality and features
- **Text fields in online forms** — critical for reducing form abandonment
- **Empty states** — explanatory copy or imagery for screens with no content yet to show
- **Loading messages** — copy shown while the user waits for processing (e.g., "Hold on tight! We're getting your results")
- **Captions** — short descriptive texts below images
- **Image alt texts** — read by screen readers and shown when images fail to load; crucial for accessibility
- **Notifications** — important information beyond regular task flows, often time-specific (e.g., upgrade available)
- **Offboarding copy** — text shown when a user leaves a product or service

Almost all types of microcopy share one trait: they **drive user action**.

### Microcopy vs. Related Terms

- **UI copy vs. UX copy:** UI refers more to visual/form aspects of an interface; UX to practical/functional aspects.
- **Microcontent:** A term used by Jakob Nielsen (NNGroup) as early as 1998 to describe headings, subheadings, preview texts, and email subject lines. Overlaps with microcopy but is not identical.
- **Marketing copy:** Fundamentally different goal. Marketing copy persuades before purchase; microcopy supports smooth product use after purchase. Some overlap exists (e.g., pre-purchase forms, upgrade prompts), but the distinction holds in practice.

### Why "Click Here" Is Not Good Microcopy

- It is not specific — users (and screen reader users in particular) have no idea where the link leads.
- Good CTA copy requires background research: knowing who the users are, where they are in the journey, what they are trying to accomplish, and how the brand voice should be applied.
- CTAs are only one small part of microcopy — it appears throughout an entire interface.

## The Early Days of Microcopy

For a long time, microcopy was a neglected part of UX design. Cryptic technical error messages were common, offering no useful information to end users.

**IMAGE MISSING** — *Example of a cryptic legacy error message with zero user-actionable information.*

**Joshua Parker** is credited with coining the term "microcopy" for text in digital interfaces. While working at User Interface Engineering, he noticed users getting stuck on a checkout page due to address entry errors. Adding the phrase "Be sure to enter the billing address associated with your credit card" solved the problem and increased revenue. He introduced the term in a blog article in 2009 — around the time the App Store (launched July 2008) was triggering explosive app growth, which in turn elevated the need for quality microcopy.

## Why Microcopy Matters

**1. It makes products easier to use.**
Clear microcopy helps users find and complete tasks. It reassures users and builds trust (e.g., by explaining data collection). Poor microcopy creates friction; unclear error messages can cause confusion, irritation, or even fear.

**2. It directly affects conversion rates.**
The right words at the right moment in a user journey can determine whether someone completes a purchase or signs up for a newsletter. This effect has been demonstrated repeatedly.

**Caveat:** Microcopy is not a miracle fix. It must be aligned with visuals, business goals, user goals, and voice and tone.

## Good Microcopy: Principles and Examples

### Anticipate User Concerns (Mental Relaxers)

Reassurance copy that addresses concerns users have before they consciously voice them. Examples:
- "You can review your order before paying"
- "You can always change this later"
- "We only send relevant updates"
- "We never share your data with third parties"
- "We hate spam as much as you do"

These give users the **confidence to proceed** — as long as the statements are true.

Even more effective: anticipating questions the user didn't know they had (e.g., informing a user on a poor connection that their saved articles are still accessible offline).

### Be Clear About the Number of Steps

Include a **progress tracker** to show users where they are in a flow and how much remains.

### Indicate the Time Commitment

Stating how long a flow will take reduces hesitation. Only use this if the time estimate is accurate — inaccurate estimates damage trust.

Examples of effective time/commitment disclosure:
- "This will only take 8 seconds"
- "Free, takes 2 minutes, no credit card required"

### Use Microcopy to Strengthen Brand Voice

Brand voice applied through microcopy builds trust and connection. Requires a well-defined voice and tone guide that specifies how copy should adapt at different points in the user journey.

## Bad Microcopy: Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Confusing Copy

Unclear CTAs that obscure the consequence of a choice. Example: a dismiss button labelled "Skip trial" when no trial was mentioned anywhere in the popup — leaves users confused and erodes trust.

**Rule:** Opt-out labels should be clear and honest. "Maybe later" is almost always better than a confusing alternative action.

### Confirmshaming

Making users feel **bad, stupid, or silly** for opting out of an offer (e.g., a decline button reading "No thanks, I don't want to improve"). Confirmshaming is high-risk, not recommended, and likely to damage brand perception.

### Excluding People

Microcopy that forces a binary gender choice (female/male only) excludes non-binary users. Before asking for sensitive information:
1. **Question whether it is needed at all.**
2. If it is needed, **explain why** it is being collected.
3. Provide **inclusive options** — and avoid labelling non-binary options as "other," which implies those users are anomalous.

## Tips for Better Microcopy

**Three core rules:**

1. **Be clear.** The most important rule. Clarity requires background research and user feedback — not just internal judgment. Avoid jargon unless you are certain the audience knows it.
2. **Be concise.** Space restrictions are one reason; cognitive load is another. Aim for a maximum of 3 words per button (per Torrey Podmajersky, *Strategic Writing for UX*) — not always possible, but a useful target.
3. **Be helpful.** Anticipate user concerns proactively.

**Additional guidance:**
- Stay true to **brand personality** and voice. Refer to or create a content style guide.
- Use a **conversational tone** — natural and human, not robotic or corporate. Clarity still takes priority over tone.

## Future Considerations for Microcopy

### Humor: Use with Caution

Humor in microcopy is genuinely difficult and context-dependent. What one user finds charming, another finds rude or tone-deaf. Mailchimp's style guide advice applies broadly: "If you're unsure, keep a straight face." A conversational tone does not require jokes. Clarity is always more important than wit.

### Inclusivity: Keep Improving

Non-inclusive microcopy affects accessibility, diversity, and gender representation. Areas to address:

- **Placeholder text contrast:** Low-contrast placeholder copy is inaccessible to users with poor eyesight. Consider making that information available through other means (e.g., persistent labels above fields).
- **Gender fields:** See anti-pattern above.
- Being creative and being inclusive are not in conflict — both are required.

### Localization: Write for Translation

Microcopy may be translated into other languages. Short, simple sentences are easier to translate accurately. Avoid:
- Cultural references
- Metaphors
- Puns

If these must be included, provide explanatory notes to the localization team.

## Conclusion

Good microcopy takes ongoing effort. It must be reviewed and revised as products evolve, and every new feature brings new microcopy to research, write, and test. Microcopy needs to be aligned with visuals, business goals, user goals, and voice and tone — it is one part of product design, not a standalone fix.
