# F-Shaped Pattern For Reading Web Content

**Note:** A follow-up eyetracking study 11 years later confirmed these findings.

Users read web pages in an F-shaped pattern: two horizontal stripes followed by a vertical stripe. In an eyetracking study of 232 users across thousands of web pages, this dominant reading pattern was consistent across many different sites and tasks.

## The F Pattern: Three Components

1. Users first read in a **horizontal movement** across the upper part of the content area — forming the F's **top bar**.
2. Users move down the page slightly, then read across in a **second horizontal movement**, typically covering a shorter area than the first — forming the F's **lower bar**.
3. Users scan the content's left side in a **vertical movement**, sometimes slow and systematic, sometimes faster and spottier — forming the F's **stem**.

Scan patterns are not always exactly three parts. Sometimes users read a third horizontal section (making the pattern more like an E); other times only one horizontal pass occurs (making it resemble an inverted L). Generally, patterns roughly resemble an F, though the distance between bars varies.

The F pattern is a rough, general shape rather than a uniform, pixel-perfect behavior. Variations include:
- **E-commerce product page:** The second crossbar sits lower than usual due to an intervening product image; significant fixation also occurs on the price and "add to cart" box in the upper right.
- **SERP:** The second crossbar is longer than the top bar because the second headline is longer than the first; both headlines attracted equal interest, though users typically read less of the second area they view.

## Implications of the F Pattern

- **Users won't read text thoroughly word-by-word.** Exhaustive reading is rare, especially during initial research phases.
- **The first two paragraphs must state the most important information.** Users are most likely to read the first paragraph; they'll read progressively less of subsequent paragraphs.
- **Start subheads, paragraphs, and bullet points with information-carrying words.** Users scanning the left-side stem of the F will read the first two words of a line far more often than the third word onward.

## Detailed Scanning Behaviors

Beyond the dominant F pattern, users exhibit other scanning behaviors depending on context — for example, a more directed scan when looking for prices or other numbers. The biggest determinant for content usability is how users read online. Because people read differently online than in print, content must be written differently.
